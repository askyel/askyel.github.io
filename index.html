Funding

7
Venture capital is one of the most valuable sources of seed money for a company, but only 2% of VC (venture capitalist) funding goes to women. This chart illustrates how VCs approach pitch meetings differently based on the gender of the founder, one with the expectation of success and the other with the expectation of failure. Notably, this is true of both male and female VCs.
Male and Female Entrepreneurs Get Asked Different Questions by VCs — and It Affects How Much Funding They Get

Lindsay Meyer, an entrepreneur in San Francisco, said Mr. Caldbeck put $25,000 of his own money into her fitness start-up in 2015. That gave Mr. Caldbeck reason to constantly text her; in those messages, reviewed by The Times, he asked if she was attracted to him and why she would rather be with her boyfriend than him. At times, he groped and kissed her, she said.“I felt like I had to tolerate it because this is the cost of being a nonwhite female founder,” said Ms. Meyer, who is Asian-American.
In a New York Times article published earlier this year, over two dozen women came forward with allegations of sexual harassment against some of the most powerful male venture capitalists in the industry. When women did protest or push back, firms often retaliated by ending funding negotiations.
Women in Tech Speak Frankly on Culture of Harrassment

Crowdfunding has become an increasingly popular alternative to raising capital for a company. While fewer women seek funding on crowdfunding sites than men, a higher percentage of female ventures reach seed goals than male ventures.

10
These two charts illustrate statistics on women-owned businesses in the United States from a 2017 report published by American Express. While the quantity of businesses owned by women has risen in the last decade, their overall share of market employment and revenue has not changed significantly. Notably, there is little overlap between the industries most popular for women-owned businesses and industries in which they scale the most.
